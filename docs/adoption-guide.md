# Adoption Guide

This guide explains how to use the Ethos Calendar without turning it into performance, surveillance, or authority.

The calendar can be used by an individual, a household, a small group, or an institution. Each setting should keep the same rule: practice must produce clearer truth, more reliable conduct, real repair, and stronger memory without coercion.

## Individual Use

Start with the [daily and weekly rule](daily-weekly-rule.md).

Minimum viable practice:

1. Use the daily rule for seven days.
2. Keep each entry to one or two sentences.
3. Complete one concrete repair during the week.
4. End the week with one keep, one retire, one repair, and one first duty for the next week.

Do not turn the practice into self-punishment. The daily rule is a contact point with reality, not a diary performance.

## Household Use

Households should use the calendar to clarify burdens, not assign blame theatrically.

Recommended monthly rhythm:

1. Review what must be maintained.
2. Name one shared duty that is falling through.
3. Name one repair that is owed.
4. Decide who will do what by when.
5. Record the decision where the household can find it.

Children can participate in age-appropriate ways, but adults remain responsible for adult burdens.

## Group Use

Groups can use the calendar for reading, practice, service, and review.

Group sessions should include:

1. One short reading.
2. One calendar question.
3. One private or shared application.
4. One bounded action.
5. One review of a previous commitment.

No participant should be required to disclose private material. A person may say "private case" and work silently or generally.

## Institutional Use

Teams, schools, nonprofits, and public-facing projects may adapt the calendar for review cycles.

Appropriate institutional uses:

- release risk reviews
- stewardship reviews
- repair days after preventable harm
- annual remembrance of founding obligations
- service and maintenance days

Institutions should not use the calendar to create loyalty tests, extract confession, or hide accountability behind symbolic observance.

## Privacy

Calendar records can contain sensitive material.

Use these defaults:

- Private individual records stay private.
- Household records should be visible only to the household unless all affected people agree otherwise.
- Group records should capture commitments and process, not forced personal disclosure.
- Institutional records should distinguish public accountability from private personnel, medical, legal, or family details.

## Stop Conditions

Stop or refer out when a matter involves:

- imminent harm
- abuse
- coercive control
- medical crisis
- legal risk
- mandated reporting
- severe mental distress
- a conflict the group has no competence to hold

The calendar can support truthful action. It must not pretend to replace professional, legal, medical, or emergency processes.
